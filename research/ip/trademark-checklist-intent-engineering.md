## 7. Descriptiveness Risk Assessment

The mark "INTENT ENGINEERING" may face a descriptiveness objection under Section 2(e)(1) of the Lanham Act. Counsel should prepare for this possibility.

### Arguments for Distinctiveness

1. **Novel combination:** While "intent" and "engineering" are common words, their combination to name a specific methodology for AI agent instruction is novel. No prior use of the combined term as a methodology name exists.

2. **Defined methodology, not generic activity:** The mark identifies a specific six-element decomposition methodology (Goal, Priority, Constraints, Failure Modes, Judgment Boundaries, Values), not a generic activity. "Intent engineering" as a named discipline did not exist before the Admiral Framework defined it.

3. **Generational lineage:** The term is positioned as the third generation in a named lineage (Prompt Engineering → Context Engineering → Intent Engineering). "Prompt engineering" has become a widely recognized named discipline; "intent engineering" follows the same naming pattern for a distinct, defined methodology.

4. **Industry adoption potential:** If the certification program launches before the Statement of Use deadline, certified practitioners using the term in a branded context would strengthen the secondary meaning argument.

### Fallback Strategy

If a descriptiveness refusal is issued:
1. Register on the Supplemental Register (provides nationwide constructive notice and ® symbol usage)
2. Build commercial evidence of secondary meaning through certification programs, published materials, and industry usage
3. After 5 years on the Supplemental Register, petition for Principal Register under Section 2(f) (acquired distinctiveness)
