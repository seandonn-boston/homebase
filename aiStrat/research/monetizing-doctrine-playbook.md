# How to Package and Sell an Operating Philosophy

**Date:** March 13, 2026

---

## The Precedents

This has been done before — repeatedly, profitably, and at massive scale. Here are the models that matter, ranked by relevance to Admiral:

---

### 1. ITIL — The Closest Analogy

**What it is:** A set of practices for IT service management. Started as a UK government specification in 1989. No software. Just doctrine.

**How it makes money:**
- PeopleCert acquired the IP for **£380 million** in 2021
- Revenue streams: certification exams ($150–$1,000+ per person), accredited training organization (ATO) licensing (2,500+ ATOs globally), official publications and training materials, translations
- Certification is **individual-only** — every IT professional pays separately
- Multi-tier certification scheme creates a progression (Foundation → Managing Professional → Strategic Leader → Master) that keeps people paying
- The framework itself is free for internal use; commercial use requires licensing

**Why it matters for Admiral:** ITIL proves that *pure doctrine* — no software product — can become a £380M asset. The business is the certification ecosystem, not the framework itself. The framework is the loss leader that creates the market.

**Key lesson:** Give the spec away. Sell the certification that proves you understand it.

---

### 2. SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) — The Certification Machine

**What it is:** A framework for scaling agile practices across large enterprises. Started as a methodology. Now a $25-35M/year business with 235 employees.

**How it makes money:**
- **Certified SAFe Practice Consultants (SPCs)** — only certified individuals can deliver SAFe training. This creates a gated supply of trainers.
- **Courseware licensing** — training organizations pay to license the official materials
- **13 certification levels** — six foundational, four intermediate, three advanced. Each requires exam fees.
- **2 million+ professionals** have been certified
- **Global partner network** across 35 countries and 350 cities
- Raised $12.5M from Eurazeo and Leeds Equity Partners

**Why it matters for Admiral:** SAFe proves a methodology company can reach $25-35M revenue with ~235 people. The key mechanic: certify the *trainers*, not just the practitioners. This creates a multiplier — every SPC trains hundreds of people, each of whom pays for certification.

**Key lesson:** Create a two-sided certification: certify the implementers (who then sell services using your framework) and certify the practitioners (who validate their skills).

---

### 3. TOGAF (The Open Group Architecture Framework) — The Standards Body Model

**What it is:** Enterprise architecture framework. Used by 80% of Global 50 companies and 60% of Fortune 500.

**How it makes money:**
- **Framework is free** for internal use → drives massive adoption
- **Individual certification** ($495–$1,200 per person)
- **Organizational membership** ($1,250+ annually, tiered by company size)
- **Commercial licensing** for training providers and consultancies
- **Tool certification** — vendors pay to get their software "TOGAF certified"
- **Training accreditation** — 70 organizations offering 71 accredited courses
- The Open Group has 900+ member organizations with combined buy-side budgets over $50B/year

**Why it matters for Admiral:** TOGAF shows the "free core, paid ecosystem" model at the enterprise architecture level. The membership model is interesting — organizations pay to influence the standard's evolution. For Admiral, this could mean enterprise members who pay to shape the doctrine.

**Key lesson:** Free adoption → massive install base → monetize the ecosystem (certifications, tool certification, membership).

---

### 4. Zero Trust — The Idea That Became an Industry

**What it is:** Not a product or a framework document. An *idea* coined by Forrester analyst John Kindervag in 2009: "never trust, always verify."

**How the idea made money:**
- Forrester monetized it through **consulting, advisory, and their existing analyst business** — the idea elevated Forrester's brand in security
- NIST codified it as **SP 800-207** (2020) → it became the implementation standard
- Entire companies built their product lines around it: **CrowdStrike, Zscaler, Palo Alto Networks, Okta** all position as "Zero Trust" vendors
- Google built **BeyondCorp** as their internal Zero Trust implementation, then productized it
- The **US government mandated it** (Executive Order 14028, 2021), creating forced demand
- Today, Zero Trust is a **multi-billion dollar market segment**

**Why it matters for Admiral:** This is the most ambitious analogy — an *operating philosophy* that created an entire market category. Kindervag didn't build software. He named a problem and articulated a principle. Others built products. He got consulting fees and speaking gigs, and Forrester got brand equity.

**Key lesson:** If you coin the vocabulary that an industry uses to think, you don't need to build the products. You become the authority that everyone references — and that authority is monetizable through advisory, certification, and brand.

## The Monetization Stack for Admiral

Based on these precedents, here's a layered approach — from fastest revenue to largest eventual value:

### Layer 1: Advisory & Consulting (Revenue in weeks)

**What:** Sell your expertise directly. Help enterprises design their agent governance.

- **Engagement types:** Governance assessments, fleet architecture reviews, decision authority mapping workshops, enforcement spectrum audits
- **Pricing:** $200-500/hr for advisory; $15K-50K for fixed-scope engagements
- **Target buyers:** Engineering leaders, platform teams, CTOs at companies deploying agent fleets
- **Why it works immediately:** You've written 15,000+ lines of doctrine. You know this space better than anyone. Companies are deploying agents *now* and have no governance playbook.

**Risk:** Doesn't scale. You're selling your time.

---

### Layer 2: Productized Knowledge (Revenue in months)

**What:** Package the doctrine into purchasable formats.

- **The Admiral Playbook** — a structured guide for implementing agent governance, with templates, checklists, decision trees, and reference architectures. Not the raw spec — a *guided implementation manual*.
- **Fleet Starter Kits** — Level 1 and Level 2 adoption packages with pre-configured agent definitions, standing orders, and enforcement templates
- **Assessment Tools** — self-service governance maturity assessments ("Where is your organization on the Admiral adoption curve?")
- **Pricing:** $500-5,000 for playbooks/kits; free tier for assessment (lead gen)

**Why it works:** This is the "franchise manual" framing from your sales pitch, made literal. You're selling the manual.

---

### Layer 3: Certification (Revenue in 6-12 months, scales indefinitely)

**What:** Create a certification program for individuals and organizations.

**Individual certifications (ITIL/SAFe model):**
- **Admiral Practitioner** — foundational understanding of the doctrine (Level 1-2 adoption)
- **Admiral Architect** — can design fleet governance for an organization (Level 3-4)
- **Admiral Certified Implementer (ACI)** — authorized to deliver Admiral implementations and training

**Organizational certifications:**
- **Admiral Compliant** — organization has implemented the framework to a defined maturity level
- **Admiral Certified Training Partner** — authorized to train others

**Pricing (based on ITIL/SAFe precedent):**
- Practitioner exam: $200-400
- Architect exam: $500-800
- ACI certification: $1,000-2,000
- Training partner accreditation: $5,000-15,000/year

**Why it works:** This is the ITIL playbook. Each person who gets certified becomes an advocate. Each training partner becomes a sales channel. Certifications also create **career incentives** — professionals want "Admiral Certified" on their LinkedIn because it signals they understand a domain that their employer desperately needs.

**The multiplier:** If even 1% of the estimated 2M+ professionals who'll be working with AI agents by 2028 get Admiral certified at $300 each, that's $6M in exam fees alone — before training partner revenue.

> **⚠️ Market Signal: Anthropic Academy (March 2, 2026)**
>
> Anthropic launched **13 free courses with certificates** at [anthropic.skilljar.com](https://anthropic.skilljar.com), covering Claude API, MCP, Agent Skills, and Claude Code. All courses are completely free with LinkedIn-ready certificates. This represents a significant counter-data-point to the paid certification model:
>
> **What it means for Admiral's certification strategy:**
> - **The "free vendor cert" precedent is now set.** Anthropic is using certifications as ecosystem demand-gen, not revenue. If developers expect AI certifications to be free, Admiral's paid tiers ($200-$800) need stronger justification.
> - **Admiral certifies a different thing.** Anthropic's certs prove you can *use their tools*. Admiral's certs would prove you can *govern agent fleets* — a higher-order, vendor-neutral skill closer to ITIL/TOGAF than to a product tutorial. This distinction is critical to defend paid pricing.
> - **Freemium hybrid may be necessary.** Consider: free Admiral Practitioner (like Anthropic's free certs — drives adoption and vocabulary spread) + paid Admiral Architect and ACI (where the governance depth justifies cost, similar to TOGAF's $495-$1,200 range).
> - **Positioning matters.** Anthropic teaches "how to build." Admiral teaches "how to govern." These are complementary, not competitive — but only if Admiral's certification clearly occupies the governance/operations lane rather than the tools/API lane.
>

### Layer 4: Ecosystem Licensing (Revenue in 12-18 months)

**What:** License the Admiral brand and IP to tools, platforms, and consultancies.

- **Tool certification:** Agent orchestration platforms (CrewAI, LangGraph, Akka) pay to be "Admiral Compatible" or "Admiral Certified" — meaning their platform supports Admiral's role architecture, decision authority model, and enforcement patterns
- **Consulting partner licensing:** Deloitte, Accenture, etc. license the right to deliver "Admiral-based governance implementations"
- **Content licensing:** Training organizations license courseware
- **Pricing:** $10K-100K/year depending on partner tier and usage rights

**Why it works:** This is the TOGAF model. Platforms *want* to be certified because their enterprise customers are asking for governance standards. Consultancies *want* to license because it gives them a structured methodology to sell.

---

### Layer 5: Standard Ownership (Long-term, highest value)

**What:** Position Admiral as the reference specification for agentic AI governance — the "NIST 800-53 for agents."

- **Submit to standards processes:** Contribute to NIST's AI Agent Standards Initiative, engage with Singapore's IMDA, participate in CSA working groups
- **Align with regulatory requirements:** Map Admiral's framework to EU AI Act obligations, OWASP Agentic Top 10, and emerging compliance mandates
- **Build the community:** Open-source the core spec, build a contributor community, create a governance board (like The Open Group)
- **Eventual structure:** Non-profit foundation owns the standard, for-profit entity runs certification and commercial licensing (the AXELOS/PeopleCert model)

**Why it works:** If Admiral becomes the standard, everything built on top references it. Every tool, every training course, every compliance audit generates demand for Admiral expertise. ITIL's IP was acquired for £380M because owning the standard means owning the ecosystem.

**The paradox:** To own the standard long-term, you may need to give it away short-term. TOGAF is free for internal use. ITIL's spec is publicly accessible. Zero Trust is just an idea anyone can implement. The monetization is in the ecosystem, not the document.

## The Core Insight

**You're not selling a document. You're selling a vocabulary, a mental model, and a certification that proves you speak it.**

The document is the seed. The business is the ecosystem that grows around it:
- ITIL's *practices* are worth £380M because millions of people organize their careers around them
- SAFe's *methodology* generates $25-35M/year because enterprises pay to certify their agile maturity
- Zero Trust's *principle* created a multi-billion dollar market because one person named a problem clearly enough that an entire industry adopted the framing

Admiral's doctrine is the same kind of asset. The question isn't "how do I sell a spec?" — it's "how do I build the ecosystem where everyone who governs AI agents speaks Admiral?"

## Doctrine IS the Product

**Updated March 14, 2026** — after comprehensive review of the competitive landscape (see `agentic-governance-frameworks-2026.md`, `idea-competitors-2026.md`)

### Why Doctrine, Not Software, Is the Right Form Factor

The competitive landscape confirms this: **every major institution (McKinsey, WEF, Singapore, NIST, CSA, Forrester, Gartner, OWASP) is publishing governance philosophy, not governance software**. The market is demanding doctrine — operating principles, frameworks, taxonomies, control catalogs — not another runtime or SDK.

Consider what already exists as software:
- **CrewAI, LangGraph, AutoGen** — agent orchestration runtimes
- **Akka** — enterprise-grade distributed agent infrastructure
- **Credo AI** — AI governance platform with agent registry
- **Gartner TRiSM vendors** — an entire market category of governance tooling

Admiral-as-software competes against all of these and loses on distribution, funding, and engineering headcount.

Admiral-as-doctrine competes against:
- McKinsey's five pillars (strategic, not implementable)
- Singapore IMDA's four dimensions (regulatory, not operational)
- CSA ATF's five trust elements (security-only)
- Forrester AEGIS's 39 controls (compliance-only)
- WEF's classification system (taxonomic, not prescriptive)

**None of them have the full stack**: strategy + context + enforcement + fleet + brain + execution + quality + operations + platform + self-calibration + protocols. Admiral is the only end-to-end operational doctrine.

### What "Doctrine as Product" Actually Means

Doctrine is not a document sitting on a shelf. Doctrine is **the guide for a system**. It tells operators:
- **What the system is** — roles, boundaries, capabilities (Fleet catalog)
- **How the system decides** — authority tiers, escalation rules, enforcement spectrum
- **How the system learns** — Brain architecture, institutional memory
- **How the system fails** — 20 failure modes with operational responses
- **How the system scales** — progressive adoption L1-L4
- **What never changes** — 15 Standing Orders, mechanically enforced

The doctrine doesn't replace software — it **governs** software. Every agent runtime needs governance. No agent runtime ships its own. That gap is the market.

### The Competitive Validation

As of March 2026, the competitive landscape validates doctrine as the right product form:

| Competitor | What they sell | What they DON'T provide (but Admiral does) |
|---|---|---|
| McKinsey | Strategic vision | Implementation specification |
| Singapore IMDA | Regulatory requirements | Operational doctrine |
| CSA ATF | Security trust model | Full operational governance |
| Forrester AEGIS | Compliance controls | Role architecture, fleet coordination, memory |
| WEF | Classification taxonomy | Enforcement mechanisms, standing orders |
| Gartner TRiSM | Market category / vendor guide | Implementable governance specification |
| OWASP | Threat taxonomy | Operational responses to threats |
| KPMG TACO | Agent type classification | Governance doctrine per type |
| CrewAI/LangGraph | Agent runtime | Agent governance |
| Akka | Distributed infrastructure | Governance specification |

**Every row has the same gap: operational doctrine.** That's Admiral.

### The Revenue Implication

Doctrine creates a **perpetual licensing surface**. Software has substitutes (switch to a competitor). Doctrine, once adopted, creates organizational lock-in:
- Roles are designed around it
- Certifications reference it
- Compliance audits map to it
- Training programs teach it
- Career paths depend on it

ITIL has been generating revenue for 37 years. Zero Trust for 17 years. The specification itself may evolve, but the *ecosystem* compounds.
