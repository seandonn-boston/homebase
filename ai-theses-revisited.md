# AI THESES — REVISITED

**What we actually believe, after arguing with ourselves.**

Created: March 2026

---

## Why This Document Exists

We wrote three thesis documents about AI: one about what it does to human skills, one about its relationship to the internet, and one about whether the investment cycle is a bubble. Each was directionally correct. Each was also too certain, too neat, and too willing to dismiss counterevidence with rhetorical moves instead of engaging with it honestly.

This document is the corrective. It states what we believe, what we're uncertain about, and what would change our minds — without the sensationalism, the cherry-picked case studies, or the false confidence that makes thesis documents read well and age poorly.

---

## Thesis 1: AI and Human Skills

### What we believe

The economic value of pure execution — writing code, producing copy, generating designs, conducting routine analysis — is declining. The direction is clear and probably irreversible. AI does not need to be better than the best human practitioner to change the economics; it only needs to be good enough and cheap enough that the gap between "expert with 10 years of experience" and "competent person with AI tools" narrows to the point where the premium for pure execution compresses.

This is happening in software engineering, where [41% of code is now AI-generated](https://github.blog/news-insights/octoverse/octoverse-2025/) and [92% of U.S. developers use AI tools daily](https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2025/). It is happening in legal research, content production, data analysis, and other fields where the output is a describable artifact.

What is *not* declining — and may be appreciating — is the value of judgment, taste, problem identification, relationships, and conviction. These are harder to automate not because of a technical limitation that will be solved next quarter, but because they require accountability, commitment, and a point of view rooted in lived experience. AI can generate options. It cannot decide which option to bet a company on and stand behind that decision when it goes wrong.

### What we're uncertain about

**The timeline.** The company-level evidence (Spotify, Anthropic, Cursor) is dramatic. The macro evidence is not. [U.S. productivity data](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/prod2.nr0.htm) shows no AI-driven spike. [Goldman Sachs found](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/generative-ai-could-raise-global-gdp-by-7-percent) AI added "basically zero" to GDP in 2025. The [CBO projects](https://www.cbo.gov/) 0.1% annual productivity gains. [89% of C-suite executives](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) report no measurable productivity impact.

We think the [Solow paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Productivity_paradox) explains the gap — electrification took 30+ years to show up in productivity stats. But if that's the right analogy, it implies the transformation takes **15–25 years**, not the 3–5 years implied by most AI commentary (including our own prior documents). The factory floor had to be physically redesigned for electricity. Organizations will need to be fundamentally redesigned for AI. That redesign has barely started.

**The depth of the disruption.** The [METR study](https://metr.org/) found experienced open-source developers were actually *slower* with AI tools on real-world tasks. This is a single study, but it contradicts the narrative that AI automatically makes everyone more productive. It may mean that bolting AI onto existing workflows produces friction, not gains — and that the real productivity comes only after workflows are redesigned around AI from scratch. If true, the disruption is real but requires far more organizational change than "give everyone Copilot."

**Whether "taste" stays human.** We believe taste and judgment are durable human advantages. But we held this belief about chess judgment, medical diagnosis, and legal reasoning too — and AI has made significant inroads in all three. RLHF-trained models are getting better at curation. The [ICSE 2026 JAWs study](https://conf.researchr.org/home/icse-2026) found human-written agent configs still outperform LLM-generated ones, but the gap may narrow. We believe taste remains human for now. We hold that belief loosely.

### What would change our mind

- Macro productivity data showing a clear AI-driven acceleration (would shorten our timeline)
- METR-style studies consistently showing AI makes experienced practitioners slower (would weaken the disruption thesis)
- AI systems that reliably identify novel, valuable problems without human framing — not just answering questions but knowing which questions to ask (would weaken the "taste is human" thesis)
- Widespread organizational redesign around AI producing measurable results at companies that are not AI-native (would confirm the direction and compress the timeline)

### Our position, plainly stated

Execution value is declining. Judgment value is rising. The direction is right. The timeline is probably 10–20 years for the full transformation, not 3–5. The people who will thrive are those who combine deep domain understanding with AI leverage — not those who had no skills and now have AI, and not those who had skills and refuse to adapt. The "one person does the work of fifty" framing is aspirational, not yet documented reality for the median knowledge worker. It may become reality. It has not yet.

## Thesis 2: AI and the Internet

### What we believe

AI adoption is faster than any prior technology wave, and the primary reason is that AI inherits the internet's distribution infrastructure. Connectivity, cloud compute, digitized knowledge, software-eaten industries, payment rails, app stores, API ecosystems — all of this was built over 30 years for internet-era reasons, and AI exploits it without having to rebuild it. [ChatGPT reached 100M users in two months](https://www.reuters.com/technology/chatgpt-sets-record-fastest-growing-user-base-analyst-note-2023-02-01/); the internet took seven years to reach the same milestone. [Enterprise AI revenue went from $1.7B to $37B in three years](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/). This velocity is only possible because the distribution problem was already solved.

This is the strongest and most well-supported version of the thesis: AI depends on internet infrastructure, and that inheritance explains the adoption speed.

### What we got wrong, or at least overstated

**Calling AI "the internet's exploitation stage" may be the wrong category.** Our original framing positioned AI as analogous to how factory redesign exploited electrification — a second stage of the same revolution. This is intellectually elegant but potentially misleading. The internet was a communication technology. AI targets cognition. These may be fundamentally different categories. If they are, internet-era pattern matching gives false comfort — the disruption could be bigger *or* smaller than the two-stage framework predicts, and the framework itself may not be the right lens.

[AlphaFold predicting 200 million protein structures](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07487-w) depends on internet infrastructure but calling it "internet exploitation" strains the framework past its useful limits. AI without the internet is a brain without a body — but brains are not merely the exploitation phase of bodies.

**The two-stage pattern is retroactive pattern-matching, not predictive law.** We originally cited electricity, railroads, and the printing press as proof of a universal infrastructure-then-exploitation pattern. But we only cited the winners. Nuclear power had massive infrastructure investment with no equivalent exploitation stage. VR has had three infrastructure cycles with no payoff. [Fiber optic cable was 85–95% unused](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot-com_bubble) years after the buildout. The pattern holds for *some* technologies. Whether AI belongs in the "electricity" category or the "nuclear power" category is a judgment call, not a proven law.

**The productivity data contradicts the "revolution is finishing" framing.** [Goldman Sachs found](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/generative-ai-could-raise-global-gdp-by-7-percent) AI added basically zero to GDP in 2025. [89% of executives](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) report no measurable productivity impact. [95% of AI pilots fail to reach production](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-11-18-gartner-says-more-than-30-percent-of-generative-ai-projects-will-be-abandoned-after-proof-of-concept-by-end-of-2025). Enterprise adoption is wide (94%) but shallow. The technology works in demos, benchmarks, and at AI-native companies. The organizational transformation required to capture value at scale is barely underway.

### What we still believe

The inheritance thesis is correct: AI's adoption speed is explained by inheriting internet infrastructure. This is the useful insight, regardless of whether you call it "stage two" or something else.

The strategic implication also holds: the winners will be companies that use AI to be dramatically better at something the internet already enabled, using existing distribution infrastructure to scale — not companies that try to build entirely new infrastructure from scratch.

### What would change our mind

- AI applications that generate massive value without depending on internet infrastructure (would disprove the inheritance thesis)
- Sustained productivity gains showing up in macro data within 2–3 years (would support the fast-timeline version we've backed away from)
- AI adoption stalling despite available infrastructure — which would suggest the bottleneck was never distribution but something else (organizational capacity, trust, regulation, actual usefulness)

### Our position, plainly stated

AI adoption is fast because it inherits internet infrastructure. That part is well-supported. Whether AI is "the internet's exploitation stage" or something categorically new is an open question we previously answered too confidently. The strategic advice — build AI-accelerated internet companies, not pure AI companies — holds either way. The timeline for full economic impact is probably 2035, not 2027. The person who says "this is a decade-long story" is probably more right than the person who says "the revolution is finishing."

## Thesis 3: AI Investment

### What we believe

This is probably not a bubble in the structural sense. The evidence for that claim is genuinely strong:

**The fundamentals are different from dotcom.** The Magnificent Seven trade at ~28x expected earnings vs. ~60x+ during the dotcom peak. Nvidia's P/E is 44–47x vs. Cisco's 472x. The Big 5 generated [$400B+ in trailing free cash flow](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/why-ai-companies-may-invest-more-than-500-billion-in-2026). These companies are funding expansion from cash flow, not debt and IPO proceeds.

**The fourth horseman is missing.** Economist [Owen Lamont's bubble-detection framework](https://fortune.com/2026/02/01/is-ai-a-bubble-top-economist-says-no-because-ipos-fraud/) requires overvaluation, bubble beliefs, share issuance, and inflows. The critical fourth — public equity issuance — is absent. U.S. companies have executed ~$1 trillion in buybacks. Share supply is shrinking, not expanding. This is structurally different from every prior bubble. (Watch OpenAI's planned Q4 2026 IPO — if it triggers a wave of AI IPOs, this changes.)

**The technology works.** The February 2026 SaaSpocalypse — [$285B in single-session market cap destruction](https://markets.financialcontent.com/stocks/article/marketminute-2026-2-6-anthropics-claude-cowork-release-triggers-285-billion-saaspocalypse-a-brutal-wake-up-call-for-legacy-tech-and-finance) across enterprise software — was caused by AI succeeding, not failing. Bubbles are characterized by technology that doesn't deliver. This technology delivered so effectively it threatened $2+ trillion in incumbent market cap.

**The entities making the bet can afford to be wrong on timing.** With $400B+ in trailing FCF, the hyperscalers can sustain years of compressed free cash flow without existential risk. Amazon ran negative FCF for years while building AWS. The question is not whether they survive, but whether the timeline to return is 3 years or 10.

### What we're uncertain about — and honest about

**The revenue gap is real, growing, and unproven to close.** [Sequoia's "$600B Question"](https://www.sequoiacap.com/article/ais-600b-question/) identified a ~$500B gap between AI infrastructure spend and AI revenue. That gap has *tripled* in 12 months, even as AI revenue has grown rapidly. [Enterprise AI revenue went from $1.7B to $37B in three years](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/) — impressive, but spending is growing faster.

Calling this gap "temporal, not structural" is the central assertion of the bull case. We lean toward it being temporal, but we must state honestly: **every bubble investor in history believed the gap between investment and returns was temporal.** Sometimes they were right (AWS). Sometimes they were wrong (fiber optics). The AI gap has fewer structural blockers than the dotcom gap (broadband exists, smartphones exist, digital-native users exist). But "fewer structural blockers" is not proof that the gap closes at the necessary rate. We believe the direction is right. We cannot prove the timeline.

**Circular financing inflates headline revenue figures.** Nvidia invests in OpenAI. OpenAI buys Nvidia chips. Microsoft invests in OpenAI. OpenAI runs on Azure. Amazon invests in Anthropic. Anthropic runs on AWS. Some portion of the revenue these companies report to each other is money going in circles. This is not proof of a bubble — corporate ecosystems always have cross-investment. But it means the *net* new economic value being created is lower than the gross revenue figures suggest. The cleanest signal of genuine demand is third-party enterprise revenue from companies with no investment relationship to the AI providers. That signal is growing, but it is smaller than the headline numbers.

**Enterprise adoption is wide but shallow.** 94% of organizations use AI. [89% of executives report no measurable productivity impact](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai). These two facts are both true, and together they are a warning: adoption and value capture are different things. The organizational transformation required to convert AI capability into economic productivity historically takes 15–25 years. Current capex commitments implicitly assume 3–5 years. That mismatch is the biggest risk to the investment thesis — not because the technology doesn't work, but because the timeline for returns may be substantially longer than the market expects.

**The AWS analogy has limits.** AWS was demand-proven — it served Amazon's own e-commerce first, then sold excess capacity. Today's AI capex is speculative build-ahead at an unprecedented scale. [$660–690 billion in 2026](https://futurumgroup.com/insights/ai-capex-2026-the-690b-infrastructure-sprint/), with [Barclays projecting](https://www.barclays.com/insights/) negative FCF for some hyperscalers through 2027–2028. "Premature in magnitude" is a very different risk profile from "fundamentally wrong" — but for investors with a 3-year horizon, the distinction may not matter much.

### What would change our mind

**Toward more bullish:**
- Revenue gap narrowing (not just revenue growing, but the *ratio* of spend-to-revenue improving)
- Third-party enterprise AI revenue growing independently of ecosystem cross-investment
- Macro productivity data showing a clear AI-driven acceleration
- [Data center vacancy rates](https://www.cbre.com/insights/reports/us-data-center-trends-2025) staying below 3%

**Toward more bearish:**
- Revenue gap continuing to widen through 2027
- Data center vacancy rates rising above 5%
- Hyperscaler capex guidance being cut
- OpenAI IPO triggering a wave of frothy AI equity issuance (the "fourth horseman" arriving)
- Enterprise AI pilot failure rates staying above 85% through 2027
- A major AI security incident that slows enterprise adoption

### Our position, plainly stated

Not a bubble. Probably a good bet. Almost certainly on a longer timeline than the market is pricing. The direction is correct. The magnitude of the bet is probably correct but may prove excessive in the near term. The timeline is almost certainly optimistic — the down payment could be 5–10 years ahead of the revenue that justifies it in full, not 3–5.

The clear-eyed position: this is a legitimate infrastructure buildout funded by the most profitable companies in history, deploying technology that demonstrably works. But "the technology works" and "the investment returns will justify this scale of spend on this timeline" are different claims. The first is proven. The second is an informed bet. We're making the bet. We know it's a bet.

## Across All Three: What We Got Wrong

The original thesis documents shared a set of rhetorical habits that we should name and retire:

**Cherry-picking case studies.** Spotify, Cursor, and Anthropic are real and impressive. They are also AI-native or developer-tool companies operating in domains uniquely suited to AI adoption. Extrapolating from them to "all knowledge work" is a leap the evidence does not yet support. We should cite them as leading indicators, not as proof of where everyone is headed.

**Dismissing counterevidence with analogies.** When the productivity data contradicted our claims, we invoked the Solow paradox. When the revenue gap was raised, we cited AWS. Analogies are useful for framing, but they are not rebuttals. The Solow paradox *also* implies a 30-year transformation. The AWS analogy *also* involved demand-proven infrastructure. Using them as dismissals of the bear case was intellectually dishonest.

**Treating "direction is right" as sufficient.** For someone making a career decision, the difference between "this happens by 2028" and "this happens by 2040" is the difference between "retrain now" and "you have time." For an investor, the difference between a 3-year and a 10-year payoff is the difference between a good trade and a bad one. Direction without timeline is not an actionable thesis.

**Overstating certainty.** "The value of doing things has collapsed." "The revolution is finishing." "The returns are coming." These are marketing sentences, not analytical ones. The calibrated versions — "execution value is declining," "the revolution is accelerating," "the returns are directionally right" — are less quotable but more honest. We should prefer honest over quotable.

---

## The Honest Summary

**On skills:** Execution value is declining. Judgment and taste are appreciating. The timeline is probably decades, not years. Learn to use the tools, but don't panic — and don't assume that because AI can do something on a benchmark, your organization will capture that capability anytime soon.

**On the internet:** AI adoption is fast because it inherits internet infrastructure. Whether that makes AI "stage two of the internet" or something categorically new is a genuinely open question. Either way, build on existing distribution rather than from scratch.

**On investment:** Not a bubble. Probably a good long-term bet. The timeline is almost certainly longer than the market expects. The revenue gap is real and unproven to close at the necessary rate. The entities making the bet can afford to be patient. The question is whether investors can.

**Across all three:** The direction is clear. The timeline is not. The person who says "this is a 2035 story" is probably more right than the person who says "this is a 2027 story." Act accordingly — which means moving decisively on direction while building tolerance for timeline uncertainty.
